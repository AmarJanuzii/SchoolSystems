How Finland's Education System Became the Best in the World

April 6, 2023

Finland's education system is successful because it prioritizes equity, invests in high-quality teacher training, trusts its teachers, uses low-stakes testing, and emphasizes play-based learning. These are all factors that other countries can learn from and potentially adopt in their own education systems.

Finland's education system has a rich history that has played a crucial role in its success. The roots of the modern Finnish education system can be traced back to the late 1800s when the country was part of the Russian Empire. At that time, the education system was largely underdeveloped, and Uno Cygnaeus, a Finnish educator and politician, recognized the need for change.

Cygnaeus believed that every child had the right to education, regardless of their social class or family background. He emphasized the importance of providing a basic education to all children and argued that education was key to Finland's future success and prosperity. His advocacy for education reform gained momentum in the early 1900s, leading to the introduction of mandatory primary education in 1921.

The mandatory primary education was a significant milestone in the development of Finland's education system. It ensured that all children had access to at least six years of formal education, regardless of their social status or family background. This was a major step towards achieving equal opportunities for all Finnish children, regardless of their socioeconomic status.

Today, Finland's education system is widely regarded as one of the best in the world. Its success can be attributed to the country's commitment to equity, its strong emphasis on teacher training and professional development, and its recognition of the importance of education as a driver of future success and prosperity.

Key Factors Contributing to Finland's Education Success

  • Focus on Equity: In Finland, every student is guaranteed equal opportunities to receive a quality education. This means that students from all backgrounds and socioeconomic statuses have access to the same level of education, regardless of their zip code.
  • Teacher Training: Finland has one of the most rigorous teacher training programs in the world. All teachers are required to have a master's degree in education, and the program emphasizes the importance of pedagogy and child development.
  • Trust in Teachers: Finland places a great deal of trust in its teachers. They are given the autonomy to design their own lessons and assessments, and they are viewed as professionals who are capable of making informed decisions about their students' education.
  • Low-Stakes Testing: Unlike many other countries, Finland does not have a culture of high-stakes testing. Instead, students are assessed through a variety of methods, including teacher-made assessments and exams that are designed to be more formative than summative.
  • Play-Based Learning: Finland places a strong emphasis on play-based learning in the early years of education. Children are encouraged to explore and learn through hands-on activities, rather than being forced to sit still and listen to lectures.

Teachers in Finland spend fewer hours at school each day and spend less time in classrooms than American teachers. Teachers use the extra time to build curriculums and assess their students. Children spend far more time playing outside, even in the depths of winter. Homework is minimal.
